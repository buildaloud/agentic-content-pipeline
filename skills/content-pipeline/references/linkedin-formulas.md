# LinkedIn Formulas

Named shapes for a LinkedIn post. The outline step picks one per post
(`postFormula` in the meta block, drawn from this file instead of
`post-formulas.md` when the medium is `linkedin`); `outline-structure-reviewer`
(outline grain) and `impact-reviewer` (draft grain) check the shape actually
holds. Same craft bar as the blog army — no AI tells; `linkedin-reviewer` and
the tone gate both run on these drafts.

Pick by what the post *is*, not by habit.

---

## hook-story-lesson
A short story with one clear takeaway. The default shape — reach for it first.
- **Hook** — the moment, stated plainly, inside the fold (see Format facts).
- **The story** — 2-4 short beats, one line each. What happened, in order.
- **The lesson** — one honest takeaway, stated flat, no moral-of-the-story bow.
- **The open** — end on a question or a flat statement that invites a reply.
- Best when: a real moment (a decision, a mistake, a small win) with one clean lesson.

## contrarian-take-thread
The common wisdom is X; here's the counter-evidence, briefly.
- **Hook** — state the common wisdom in one line, undercut it in the next.
- **The evidence** — 1-2 concrete beats from real work, not a listicle.
- **The nuance** — when the common wisdom is right (keeps it honest, not clickbait).
- **The open** — a direct question or flat closing line.
- Best when: real first-hand evidence against a popular belief.

## build-log-snapshot
A single, specific update from work in progress — not a full story, a snapshot.
- **Hook** — the one fact that matters ("shipped the thing that's been broken for 3 weeks").
- **The detail** — one or two concrete specifics (a number, a screenshot description, a decision).
- **What's next** — one line, no roadmap dump.
- **The open** — a question or flat statement.
- Best when: an in-progress build with one real, specific update — not a status report.

## numbers-post
A result, stated with real numbers, and what produced it.
- **Hook** — the number, up front, with just enough context to make it land.
- **How** — the 1-2 real decisions that produced the number, not a full retrospective.
- **The catch** — one honest limitation or caveat (numbers-only posts read like ads without this).
- **The open** — a question or flat statement.
- Best when: a real, verifiable number worth sharing on its own.

## Rules for every formula
- The hook must fully land inside the ~200-210 characters LinkedIn shows before
  the "see more" cutoff — see `references/agents/linkedin-reviewer.md` for the
  exact check.
- One idea per post. LinkedIn rewards a single clear point, not a survey.
- End on a question that invites a real answer, or a flat, confident statement —
  never a clichéd "thoughts?" or "agree?" tacked on for engagement's sake.
- Formula is a skeleton, not a cage — break a beat when the material demands it,
  but know you're breaking it.

## Format facts (hard rules, not style choices)
- **Above-the-fold cutoff:** only ~200-210 characters show before LinkedIn's
  "see more" truncates the post. The hook must make its full promise inside
  that window — don't split the hook's payoff across the fold.
- **Length zone:** ~1,300 characters is the high-engagement sweet spot; 3,000
  characters is the hard platform max. Longer isn't better past the sweet spot.
- **Links: contested, not banned.** LinkedIn denies penalizing link posts and
  gives you link-engagement analytics; some third-party algorithm studies have
  reported lower average reach on posts with links. The "put it in the first
  comment" workaround is widely considered obsolete. Default guidance: a link
  is fine — keep it to one, place it after the hook has landed (never before),
  and let your own per-post analytics settle the question for your account.
- **Whitespace, not walls.** Short lines, one-line paragraphs, real line breaks
  between beats. A LinkedIn post is read on a phone, one thumb-scroll at a time.
- **No markdown rendering.** LinkedIn shows literal `**`/`#`/`-` characters, not
  bold/headers/bullets. Write plain text; use unicode bullets (•) or em-dashes
  sparingly for structure if needed, never markdown syntax.
- **No engagement-bait closers.** Skip "thoughts?", "agree?", "am I wrong?" as a
  reflexive tack-on. A real, specific question or a confident flat statement
  does more work.

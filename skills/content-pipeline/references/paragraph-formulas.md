# Paragraph Formulas

Named paragraph shapes. Each beat in a post should be one of these — a paragraph
with a job, not filler. The `impact-reviewer` (formerly `section-impact-reviewer`)
names the shape of each beat; a beat with no shape is usually the flat one to
cut or rewrite.

---

## hook
Earn the next line. One concrete, specific opener — a symptom, a number, a claim.
No warm-up, no "in this post". *"Both endpoints 503'd, but only on the custom domain."*

## claim-then-evidence
State a point, then back it immediately with a real detail (number, code, quote).
The evidence is what makes it land; a claim with no evidence is an opinion.

## turn
The pivot. "I thought X. Then I found Y." Reversal is the engine of a war-story;
use it where the story actually turned, not decoratively.

## show-the-work
Walk the concrete steps/commands/decisions. Earns trust by being specific enough
to reproduce. Keep it to the steps that carry meaning, not a transcript.

## payoff
The resolution or result — what changed, stated plainly. Follows a build-up;
don't pay off a tension you never built.

## honest-limit
Name what the thing *doesn't* do, or what's still rough. One sentence. This is
where technical honesty lives (see the bullshit-detector). Builds more trust than
any superlative.

## aside
A short, human beat — a dry joke, a real reaction, a one-line tangent. Texture,
used sparingly. Too many and the post reads try-hard; none and it reads like docs.

## cta
The one action you want the reader to take, in the configured voice, as the last
substantive beat. Specific ("add the tool with one command"), not "let me
know what you think".

---

## Rules
- One job per paragraph. If a paragraph does two jobs, split it.
- Vary the shapes — a run of five claim-then-evidence paragraphs reads flat.
- Emotion/interest comes from turn, honest-limit, and aside — a post that's all
  show-the-work is correct and boring. The reviewer flags that.
- Short paragraphs. Concrete over abstract. Real numbers over adjectives.
